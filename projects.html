üìä Manufacturing Digital Twin

Orizon Aerostructures | Summer 2024

Project Overview

Digital twins are transforming modern manufacturing, and I experienced this transformation firsthand at Orizon Aerostructures, a Tier 1 aerospace manufacturer producing 75% of F-35 components and wingboxes for Boeing aircraft. Recognizing critical inefficiencies in production visibility across our manufacturing floor, I researched digital twin technology and designed a comprehensive implementation strategy to bridge the gap between our physical operations and digital systems.

The Challenge

Our manufacturing floor faced a fundamental problem: lack of real-time visibility into part location, production status, and quality metrics. This created several cascading issues:

  • Delayed delivery forecasts due to manual part tracking
  • Limited visibility into production bottlenecks
  • Reactive (rather than predictive) quality control
  • Disconnected data between ERP systems and shop floor reality

I identified that real-time data capture was the critical missing piece preventing us from leveraging digital twin technology.

My Solution

I designed a multi-layered digital twin implementation strategy combining IoT sensors, computer vision, and data integration:

üé• Vision-Based Part Tracking

Deployed camera-equipped Raspberry Pi systems at each manufacturing station to scan QR codes on parts, enabling real-time location tracking and accurate delivery forecasting.

üîß Smart Tooling Integration

Proposed sensor-equipped tooling (smart drills with torque and position monitoring) to capture quality metrics in real-time, creating a foundation for predictive quality control.

üìä Data Pipeline Architecture

Built SQL ‚Üí Power BI pipeline integrating ERP and floor data, transforming raw manufacturing data into actionable real-time insights.

üéØ Continuous Improvement Loop

Established feedback mechanisms enabling data-driven process optimization and predictive maintenance scheduling.

Impact & Results

Through this implementation, I witnessed firsthand how digital twins can transform manufacturing operations:

  • Reduced rework through real-time quality monitoring and early defect detection
  • Optimized throughput by identifying and eliminating production bottlenecks
  • Enabled data-driven decision-making across production planning and resource allocation
  • Improved delivery accuracy with real-time part tracking and forecasting

Technologies Used

Power BI SQL Raspberry Pi Python IoT Sensors Computer Vision ERP Integration Real-Time Data Processing

Key Learnings

This project solidified my understanding that digital twins aren't just about technology‚Äîthey're about fundamentally changing how manufacturing operations work. The key to successful implementation is:

  • Starting with real problems rather than technology solutions
  • Building incremental capabilities that deliver immediate value
  • Ensuring data quality and accessibility at every layer
  • Creating actionable insights that drive behavioral change

This experience showed me the transformative potential of digital twins and reinforced my commitment to building the next generation of industrial operations platforms.

SAE Aero UW | December 2023 - Present

From Zero to Flight: Building UW Madison's SAE Aero Team

I founded SAE Aero UW with a vision: to design, build, and fly competition RC aircraft that could compete on the national stage. What started as a handful of passionate students has grown into a 40+ member organization that represents UW Madison at the international SAE Aero Design Competition. This is the story of how we turned failure into success, and ambition into results.

The Journey

Spring 2023 - The Beginning

Founded SAE Aero UW with a small team of dedicated students. We spent the entire spring semester on our initial aircraft design, learning CAD modeling, aerodynamics calculations, and structural analysis. Every decision was new territory‚Äîairfoil selection, material choices, manufacturing methods.

Summer 2023 - First Build

Committed my summer to building our first aircraft. Working solo in the shop, I fabricated the wings, fuselage, and control surfaces. By summer's end, we had a complete aircraft ready for flight testing. The build taught me invaluable lessons about manufacturing tolerances, assembly procedures, and the reality of turning CAD into physical hardware.

Fall 2023 - The Crash

Our first flight test was a spectacular failure. Insufficient lift at takeoff speed sent our aircraft tumbling off the runway. It was crushing‚Äîmonths of work destroyed in seconds. But failure is where learning begins. We analyzed the data: our lift calculations were off, our wing loading was too high, and our motor selection was inadequate.

But something unexpected happened: The crash generated buzz. Students wanted to be part of a team brave enough to fail publicly and get back up. Our team exploded to 20+ members practically overnight.

2023-2024 - Scaling Up

With our expanded team, we set an ambitious goal: compete at SAE Aero Design and fly successfully. This wasn't just a bigger aircraft‚Äîit was a complete organizational challenge:

  • Technical complexity: More sophisticated design with increased payload capacity
  • Extended timeline: Design, fabrication, testing, and iteration cycles
  • Major fundraising: Secured ~$15,000 for prototypes, materials, travel, and competition entry
  • Team management: Coordinated 40+ students across design, manufacturing, testing, and business teams

The year was a grind. Endless nights in the shop, design reviews, manufacturing setbacks, and constant iteration. But the team was unified by a single goal: fly at competition.

Spring 2024 - We Flew!

Second flight test: engines roared, the aircraft accelerated down the runway, and then‚Äîliftoff. We flew! After a year of setbacks and grinding work, we had a competition-ready aircraft. We made final refinements and shipped everything to Van Nuys, California.

SAE Aero Design Competition 2024

We competed against teams from around the world in Van Nuys. While we placed in the back half of the field, we achieved our primary goal: flying at competition. For a team that didn't exist 18 months earlier, and that had crashed spectacularly just months before, this was a monumental success.

The experience was invaluable‚Äîwe learned what it takes to compete at the highest level, identified our weaknesses, and returned to Madison with clear goals for improvement.

2024-2025 - Optimization Year

Now we're focused on eliminating inefficiencies and competing among the top teams in the world. We've restructured our design process, improved our manufacturing workflows, and implemented lessons learned from competition. The goal: top 10 finish at SAE Aero Design 2025.

üöÅ Autonomous Drone Interceptor

Senior Design Project | September 2025 - Present | 5-Person Team

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem: Cost Asymmetry in Naval Defense

Cargo ships in the Red Sea are being targeted by Houthi Samad 2 drones (~$20,000 each). Current defense systems use SM-2 missiles (~$2,000,000 each), creating a massive 100:1 cost asymmetry that favors attackers. The US and allies are spending 100 times more per intercept than the cost of each threat, creating an economically unsustainable defense posture.

üí∞ Threat Cost

$20,000 - Houthi Samad 2 drone cost. Attackers can afford to launch dozens of drones, overwhelming expensive defenses through sheer volume.

üöÄ Current Defense Cost

$2,000,000 - SM-2 missile defense cost per intercept. This asymmetry is economically unsustainable and strategically dangerous.

‚úÖ Our Solution Cost

$5,000 - $10,000 - Our autonomous interceptor cost. A 200x cost reduction while maintaining kinetic effectiveness.

üéØ Strategic Impact

Restoring cost parity in naval defense, enabling sustainable protection of commercial shipping lanes against asymmetric threats.

üí° Our Solution: Cost-Effective Kinetic Interceptor

Our team is designing an autonomous quadcopter interceptor that launches from ships to kinetically destroy incoming drones at a fraction of current defense costs. This is a one-time-use system optimized for high-speed interception at close range (1-2 km), bridging the gap between expensive missile defense and affordable threats.

‚ö° 100 MPH Flight Speed

High-speed interception capability enabling engagement of fast-moving targets within the 1-2 km interception window.

üì¶ 2 lbs Total Weight

Lightweight design with payload, optimized for maximum acceleration and maneuverability during terminal approach.

üéØ 1-2 km Intercept Range

Short-range system launched when threat reaches ~5 km, with actual interception at 1-2 km for maximum accuracy.

ü§ñ Autonomous + FPV Guidance

ArduPilot autonomous navigation to intercept coordinates, with Raspberry Pi computer vision for terminal guidance accuracy.

‚öôÔ∏è How It Works: 4-Step Interception Process

Step 1: Detection & Calculation

Ground radar system detects incoming Samad 2 drone at approximately 5 km range. Python script calculates intercept coordinates based on threat trajectory, speed, and timing. System computes optimal launch window and intercept point for autonomous navigation.

Step 2: Launch & Autonomous Flight

Interceptor launches from ship toward calculated intercept coordinates. ArduPilot flight controller navigates autonomously using GPS waypoint guidance. Flight time: approximately 2 minutes maximum, with intercept typically occurring at 1-2 km from ship.

Step 3: Terminal Guidance (Computer Vision)

As interceptor approaches target zone, Raspberry Pi-based computer vision system activates. FPV camera scans for target using object detection algorithms. System transitions from GPS waypoint navigation to visual tracking for final approach, ensuring high-accuracy terminal guidance at 100 mph closing speed.

Step 4: Kinetic Interception

Interceptor physically collides with threat at 100 mph, destroying both aircraft through kinetic impact. One-time-use system achieves mission objective at 0.25-0.5% of SM-2 missile cost, restoring economic viability to naval defense operations.

üîß Technical Challenges

This project pushes the boundaries of autonomous drone technology in several critical areas:

Software Integration

Seamlessly bridging ArduPilot autonomous navigation with Raspberry Pi computer vision requires sophisticated handoff logic, fail-safes, and real-time decision-making algorithms. The transition must be instantaneous and reliable.

Power Distribution

Achieving 100 mph flight speed demands maximum motor current while preventing motor failure and maintaining 2-minute flight time. Balancing power delivery, thermal management, and weight constraints is critical.

Flight Stability at High Speed

Maintaining stable flight at 100 mph requires precise center-of-gravity calculations, aerodynamic optimization, and flight angle management. Small errors compound rapidly at high speeds.

Interception Accuracy

Hitting a moving target at 100 mph closing speed demands sub-second reaction times, precise visual tracking algorithms, and robust object detection. Terminal guidance must work flawlessly on first attempt.
